THE PARADOX OF INFLUENCE

Exploring the Impact of Over-Endorsement and Authenticity on Consumer Engagement Intent in Social Media Marketing
JP
Jay Priyadarshi
MBA in Marketing · Ganpat University, Gujarat · jaypriyadarshi23@gnu.ac.in
Abstract
This study investigates the complex relationship between over-endorsement, authenticity, and consumer engagement in the context of influencer marketing on social media. Using a mixed-methods approach, data from 443 respondents reveal that over-endorsement — when influencers promote too many products — negatively impacts key factors such as perceived relatability, emotional connection, and trust, ultimately reducing consumer engagement. However, the study also identifies authenticity as a moderating factor that can help sustain consumer engagement, even in the presence of over-endorsement. Furthermore, cultural alignment plays a significant role in shaping consumer perceptions and engagement, especially in culturally diverse markets like Gujarat, India. The findings provide actionable insights for brands and influencers, highlighting the importance of maintaining a careful balance between endorsement frequency and authenticity to build trust, preserve emotional connections, and foster long-term consumer engagement.
Keywords
Over-EndorsementPerceived RelatabilityAuthenticityEmotional ConnectionTrustCultural AlignmentEngagement Intent
1. Introduction

The rise of social media has fundamentally reshaped how brands communicate with consumers. Influencer marketing — once a niche tactic — has become a primary channel for brand discovery, credibility-building, and direct conversion. Platforms like Instagram, YouTube, and emerging short-form video apps have given individual creators unprecedented access to audiences that were once only reachable through mass media.

Yet something curious is happening in this landscape. As influencer marketing has scaled, so has the frequency with which individual influencers endorse multiple, sometimes unrelated, products within compressed timeframes. Consumers — particularly younger, digitally-native audiences — have become increasingly sensitive to the difference between an influencer who genuinely uses a product and one who is simply being paid to mention it.

This study terms that condition over-endorsement, and explores its consequences on consumer engagement intent. The central question is not whether influencer marketing works — the commercial evidence suggests it does, at scale — but whether there is a threshold beyond which the volume and frequency of endorsements begins to corrode the very trust that made the channel effective in the first place.

If a creator endorses five products in a single week, does that reduce how much a consumer trusts their recommendation of any one of them? And if so — what is the role of authenticity in moderating that erosion?
2. Research Objectives

This study pursues four primary objectives. First, to examine the direct relationship between over-endorsement and consumer engagement intent on social media platforms. Second, to investigate how perceived authenticity moderates the impact of over-endorsement on consumer trust and engagement. Third, to understand the role of emotional connection and perceived relatability as mediating variables. Fourth, to explore how cultural context — specifically the values and social norms of consumers in Gujarat, India — shapes these dynamics in ways that may differ from Western-centric research.

3. Literature Review

Influencer marketing is rooted in a simple mechanic: parasocial relationships. Consumers develop a sense of familiarity and trust with creators they follow regularly, even without direct interaction. This relationship, when genuine, creates a form of peer recommendation that traditional advertising cannot replicate. However, parasocial trust is not unconditional. Research in consumer psychology suggests that perceived authenticity — the extent to which a consumer believes an influencer is expressing genuine beliefs rather than performing for commercial incentive — is the primary variable determining whether an endorsement is effective or counterproductive.

Prior literature on advertising fatigue, source credibility theory, and persuasion knowledge provides a theoretical foundation for understanding over-endorsement. When consumers become aware that a recommendation is motivated by commercial incentive rather than genuine belief, they activate what is called persuasion knowledge — a cognitive defence mechanism that critically evaluates and often discounts the message. The more frequently this mechanism is triggered, the more systematically a consumer distances themselves from the influencer's recommendations.

What existing literature has not adequately addressed is the intersecting role of cultural alignment in markets where collectivist values, family influence, and community trust play a larger role in purchase decisions than individual endorsement. Indian consumers — particularly those in culturally cohesive states like Gujarat — may respond differently to perceived inauthenticity than Western counterparts who place higher value on individual expression.

4. Methodology

The study employed a mixed-methods approach, combining a structured online survey with follow-up qualitative interviews. The quantitative component captured responses from 443 participants across age groups 18–45, all active social media users who followed at least two influencers. The survey instrument measured perceptions of over-endorsement, authenticity, trust, emotional connection, and behavioural engagement intent using validated Likert-scale items adapted from established consumer behaviour instruments.

Qualitative interviews (n=22) were conducted to surface nuances that quantitative data alone could not capture — particularly around cultural identity, the role of aspiration versus relatability, and how consumers negotiate their own scepticism about influencer content.

5. Key Findings
01
Over-endorsement directly reduces trust. Consumers exposed to influencers who endorsed 5+ products weekly reported significantly lower trust scores than those following influencers with 1–2 weekly endorsements.
02
Authenticity is the critical moderator. Even among high-frequency endorsers, influencers perceived as authentic maintained substantially higher engagement intent scores — reducing the negative effect of over-endorsement by approximately 38%.
03
Emotional connection mediates engagement. Perceived relatability and emotional connection to an influencer significantly mediated the relationship between endorsement frequency and purchase intent.
04
Cultural alignment amplifies the effect. In the Gujarat sample, influencers who reflected local values, language, and aspirational contexts saw higher trust resilience despite over-endorsement.
6. Discussion

The findings point to what might be called the paradox of influence at scale: the very growth of an influencer's commercial activity tends to erode the authenticity that made them influential in the first place. Brands that seek influencers primarily for reach metrics without considering endorsement frequency may be investing in declining returns.

The best influencers are not the ones who say yes to the most brands. They are the ones whose audience never questions why they said yes.

The cultural dimension adds important nuance. In Gujarat, consumers responded positively to influencers who embedded product endorsements within lifestyle narratives consistent with local values — family, community, achievement through integrity. Generic lifestyle content that reflected aspirational Western aesthetics without cultural grounding saw faster trust erosion among this demographic.

7. Implications for Brands & Creators
StakeholderImplication
BrandsPrioritise authenticity fit over reach metrics. Fewer, well-aligned partnerships outperform high-frequency endorsement campaigns.
InfluencersGuard your credibility actively. Selective endorsement is not just ethical — it compounds in commercial value over time.
MarketersBuild cultural alignment into influencer selection criteria. Regional market resonance is not a soft metric — it directly affects conversion probability.
PlatformsTransparency features may paradoxically support trust if paired with authentic narrative context from the creator.
8. Conclusion

This study confirms that over-endorsement is a measurable and significant threat to influencer marketing effectiveness, mediated through trust, emotional connection, and perceived relatability. Authenticity is the most powerful buffer against this erosion — and cultural alignment amplifies the protective effect of authenticity in regionally cohesive markets.

The paradox of influence is not that attention is scarce — it is that trust is. In an environment of abundant content and limited credibility, the influencers and brands that will endure are those who treat trust not as a byproduct of popularity, but as the primary asset to be protected, carefully and consistently, over time.

Jay Priyadarshi · MBA in Marketing, Ganpat University, Gujarat, India · jaypriyadarshi23@gnu.ac.in · Published: September 2025

Why the Best Partnership Deals Start With Listening, Not Pitching

JP
Jay Priyadarshi
Business Partnerships Manager · Writer

There is a version of sales that most of us were trained on — even if no one called it training. It goes something like this: you prepare your deck, you refine your pitch, you book the meeting, and then you spend forty-five minutes explaining why what you offer is exactly what the other person needs. You bring energy. You handle objections. You close.

I spent enough time in courtrooms to know that this approach has a name in advocacy circles too: it is called arguing before you have understood the case. In law, that is malpractice. In partnerships, it is just very common.

The Pitch Is a Defence Mechanism

I do not think people pitch because they are arrogant. I think they pitch because they are nervous. A prepared pitch is a controlled environment. You know the material. You have rehearsed the transition from problem to solution. You feel capable, even confident.

But here is what a pitch does to the person sitting across from you: it signals that you have already decided what they need. And the moment a person feels that, they stop being a participant in a conversation and start being a target of a presentation. They may listen politely. They will rarely commit deeply.

The most dangerous moment in a partnership conversation is when you are talking instead of learning.

I had a meeting early in my partnerships work where I had prepared extensively. I knew the prospect's industry, their likely pain points, the relevant case studies. I was ready. And in the first ten minutes of that call, they told me something that invalidated half of my preparation — their real problem was something I had not considered at all. Because I had actually listened to those first ten minutes instead of using them to mentally cue my next slide, the meeting changed. We ended up talking for two hours. The deal closed three weeks later.

What Real Listening Does to a Deal

When you genuinely listen — not strategic active listening, not mirroring techniques, but actual curiosity about what the other person is trying to solve — several things happen that no pitch can manufacture.

First, you get real information. Most salespeople have access to the same public information about a company. But the person sitting across from you has the private information — the internal pressure they are under, the previous vendor who disappointed them, the stakeholder whose approval they need to navigate. That information only comes out if they trust you enough to share it. And they will not trust you if you are waiting for them to finish speaking so you can make your point.

Second, you build a different kind of rapport. There is a version of rapport that is manufactured — the mirrored body language, the remembered detail about their hometown, the calculated pause before responding. It works, sometimes. But it does not compound. Genuine curiosity compounds. People can feel the difference, and they remember it when they decide who they want to work with long-term.

Third, you earn the right to propose. A proposal that follows genuine understanding feels collaborative rather than transactional. You are not selling them something — you are offering a solution you have constructed together, from the inside out.

The One Shift That Changes Everything

The practical change is this: go into every partnership conversation with the explicit goal of leaving knowing something you did not know when you arrived. Not a prospect to be converted. A person with a context you do not yet fully understand. Your job in the first meeting is not to close — it is to understand clearly enough that whatever you eventually propose actually fits.

This requires tolerating silence instead of filling it. Asking a follow-up question when the instinct is to transition to your proof point. Being willing to say, at the end of a first meeting, "I don't think I know enough yet to propose the right thing — can we talk again next week?"

That sentence has opened more doors for me than any pitch I have ever delivered.

The deals I am most proud of are not the ones I closed fastest. They are the ones where the person on the other side later told me that the process of working with me to reach a decision was itself a signal of what working with me would be like. The partnership had already begun before anything was signed.

A Final Thought

My background in law taught me that the best advocates are not those who speak most eloquently — they are those who understand the facts most completely. Understanding takes precedence over persuasion, always. The argument is only as strong as the foundation beneath it.

Partnerships are not much different. The proposal is only as compelling as the understanding that shaped it. And that understanding begins not when you start talking, but when you stop.

Jay Priyadarshi · Business Partnerships Manager · Ahmedabad, India
